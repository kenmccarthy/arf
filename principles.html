Five Core Principles

Assessment redesign in an AI-enhanced context should be guided by a clear set of educational principles that safeguard academic standards while supporting meaningful learning.

Principle 1: Validity

Validity means ensuring that assessment tasks genuinely measure the knowledge, skills, and competencies they are intended to assess, even in contexts where AI tools are widely available.

What This Means

  • Assessment tasks accurately reflect the intended learning outcome
  • The task cannot be compromised or completed through inappropriate use of AI
  • The evidence gathered genuinely demonstrates student capability
  • The assessment remains rigorous and meaningful

How to Apply It

  • Critically examine whether your current assessment can be outsourced to AI
  • If yes, redesign to make learning process visible (drafts, explanations, oral components)
  • Align assessment methods with disciplinary standards and professional requirements
  • Use varied assessment formats to capture different types of learning

Principle 2: Fairness and Equity

Fairness and equity means recognising differences in access to technology, levels of AI literacy, language background, and learning needs, and designing assessment that does not disadvantage particular groups of students.

Key Equity Considerations

  • Access to AI tools: Not all students have equal access to quality AI tools or data plans
  • AI literacy: Students come from different educational backgrounds with varying AI familiarity
  • Language background: EAL students may find written reflections or oral presentations differently challenging
  • Neurodiversity: Timed or synchronous components may disadvantage some learners
  • Disability: Physical or sensory disabilities require accessible materials and alternatives

How to Apply It

  • Avoid assessment designs that privilege those with better access to technology
  • Offer multiple formats for demonstrating the same learning outcome
  • Build flexibility into deadlines and submission methods where possible
  • Explicitly teach assessment formats rather than assuming students know them
  • Regularly review assessment outcomes disaggregated by student demographic
Key Point: Good assessment design for students with disabilities or different backgrounds is good assessment design for all students.

Principle 3: Transparency

Transparency means clearly communicating expectations around the purpose of assessment, criteria for success, and the extent to which AI use is permitted or restricted.

What This Includes

  • Clear assessment briefs: Students understand what the task is asking and why
  • Explicit criteria: Rubrics or grading standards are visible in advance
  • AI use expectations: Whether AI is forbidden, permitted, or encouraged‚Äîand why
  • Feedback mechanisms: How and when students will receive feedback
  • Academic integrity policies: What constitutes appropriate and inappropriate practice

How to Apply It

  • Provide detailed assessment briefs with learning outcomes explicitly stated
  • Explain the educational purpose: "This assessment requires independent work because..."
  • Use consistent language about AI use across modules in your programme
  • Provide examples of strong work so students can visualise expectations
  • Hold Q&A sessions early in the assessment period to clarify expectations

Principle 4: Accessibility and Inclusion

Accessibility and inclusion means aligning assessment design with Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles and offering diverse ways for students to demonstrate their learning.

UDL in Assessment

  • Multiple means of representation: Provide information in various formats (text, video, visual, audio)
  • Multiple means of expression: Allow students to demonstrate learning through different formats (essay, presentation, portfolio, dialogue)
  • Multiple means of engagement: Connect assessment to student interests and values where possible

Practical Examples

  • Offer choice of essay, presentation, or digital portfolio for the same outcome
  • Provide transcripts for video or audio content
  • Allow use of assistive technology (text-to-speech, speech-to-text)
  • Build in time flexibility rather than rigid deadlines
  • Clearly explain assessment expectations rather than assuming prior knowledge

How to Apply It

  • Review assessment designs with accessibility in mind
  • Consult with disability services early
  • Ensure materials are in accessible formats (large print, digital, etc.)
  • Make accommodation a base design feature, not an add-on

Principle 5: Alignment with Learning Outcomes

Alignment with learning outcomes means ensuring that decisions about assessment format, level of supervision, and AI use are driven by what students are expected to know, understand, and be able to do.

What This Means

Every assessment decision should answer: "Does this assessment genuinely measure the intended learning outcome?"

  • Choose assessment formats that best measure the specific outcome
  • Avoid "traditional" assessment just because it's familiar
  • Match the level of supervision to what's being assessed
  • Consider whether AI use supports or undermines the outcome

Example Decision Tree

Learning Outcome: Students can demonstrate independent understanding of clinical assessment procedures.

Question: Can this be done via essay with AI permitted? Probably not.

Better approach: Supervised clinical simulation or oral defence of clinical reasoning.

Learning Outcome: Students can critically evaluate AI outputs and identify limitations.

Question: Should students use AI? Yes‚Äîit's the point of the assessment.

Approach: Analyse AI-generated content, document findings, explain why outputs are/aren't trustworthy.

How to Apply It

  • Start assessment design from learning outcomes, not assessment formats
  • Ask: "What's the best way to verify students can do this?"
  • Align format, supervision level, and AI permissions to the outcome
  • Discuss with colleagues: Is this outcome best assessed individually or collaboratively? Orally or written? Supervised or open?

AI Literacy: Core Development Outcome

Assessment redesign and AI literacy are intrinsically linked. As Gen AI becomes embedded in learning, work, and everyday life, students need more than technical familiarity with tools ‚Äî they need the capacity to engage with AI critically, ethically, and effectively.

Assessment plays a key role not only in measuring disciplinary knowledge but in developing students' ability to navigate AI-saturated environments responsibly.

What AI Literacy Includes

  • Critical evaluation: Recognising inaccuracies, bias, hallucinations, and limitations in AI outputs
  • Contextual judgment: Understanding how and when AI can be appropriately used, and when independent thinking or professional judgement is required
  • Ethical use: Using AI transparently, disclosing use, respecting academic integrity and data privacy
  • Integration as support: Using AI for thinking, problem-solving, and creativity‚Äînot as a substitute for learning
  • Human-AI understanding: Recognising the role of the human in learning, judgment, and ethical oversight
  • Influence awareness: Understanding how AI shapes thinking, behaviour, and decision-making

At Programme Level

Teams should determine:

  • Where AI literacy is an explicit learning outcome
  • Where AI literacy contributes to broader attributes (critical thinking, digital competence, ethical awareness)
  • Where independent knowledge or professional standards must be demonstrated without AI support
Central Point: Assessment redesign is not only about preventing misuse‚Äîit's about educating students to become informed, critical, and responsible participants in an AI-enhanced world.

In Practice: Applying the Principles

These principles guide how you redesign assessment. In practice, this means placing greater emphasis on:

Higher-Order Thinking Skills

  • Analysis
  • Evaluation
  • Synthesis
  • Creation

Authentic Application

  • Real-world contexts
  • Discipline-relevant problems
  • Professional practice
  • Practical application

Demonstration of Reasoning

  • Show the thinking process, not just final products
  • Include reflections on decisions made
  • Explain choices and alternatives considered
  • Make learning process visible

Critical Question for All Assessments

Does the use of Gen AI in this assessment support or undermine the intended learning outcome?

Where AI use is appropriate, expectations should be explicit and supported through the development of student AI literacy. Where independent performance is required, this should be clearly justified and communicated.
