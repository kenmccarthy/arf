Tips for Redesigning Your Assessments

These practical tips will help you redesign one assessment or a whole course:

1

Start with Learning Outcomes

Clearly define what you are assessing â€” application of knowledge, critical thinking, problem-solving, analytical or evaluative skills. Determine whether GenAI use supports or undermines those outcomes.

2

Be Explicit About AI Permissions

Clearly communicate whether learners are permitted to use GenAI and, if so, to what extent and for which purposes. Where expectations cannot be monitored, redesign the task to make learning visible.

3

Balance Higher-Order Thinking with Foundational Knowledge

Design assessments that prioritise analysis, evaluation, and creation, while ensuring students demonstrate essential disciplinary knowledgeâ€”sometimes independently of AI support.

4

Use Multi-Faceted Assessment Formats

Combine artefact-based tasks with lower-risk approaches such as oral presentations, peer evaluation, drafts, reflective journals, or viva-style discussions to verify understanding.

5

Assess the Process, Not Only the Product

Build in touchpoints or staged components where each part of assessment develops from the previous. Make student thinking visible through drafts, reflections, and interactive elements.

6

Incorporate Structured Collaboration

Include opportunities for collaborative problem-solving, peer feedback, or group projects with mechanisms to recognise individual contributions. This reflects authentic practice and makes learning harder to outsource.

7

Engage Students in Dialogue About Assessment

Facilitate open conversations about GenAI use, academic integrity, and responsible practice. Involve learners in assessment design when appropriateâ€”discussing criteria or co-creating rubric elements.

Applying the Tips

These tips work best when used together. For example:

  • Tip 1 + Tip 2: Identify your learning outcome, then decide whether AI use supports it
  • Tip 3 + Tip 5: Balance foundational knowledge with process visibility through staged submissions
  • Tip 4 + Tip 6: Use multiple formats, including collaboration, to make cheating harder

Implementation Guidance

Phased Approach to Assessment Redesign

Rather than redesigning everything at once, consider a phased approach:

Year 1: Foundation

  • Audit current assessments using risk table
  • Identify 2-3 high-risk assessments per module
  • Redesign one high-risk assessment per module as pilot
  • Gather feedback from students and staff
  • Share learning across programme

Year 2-3: Expansion

  • Roll out successful approaches across all modules
  • Refine based on Year 1 feedback
  • Implement programme-level coordination
  • Develop staff confidence and expertise
  • Integrate into quality assurance processes

Starting Small

You don't need to redesign everything at once. Consider starting with:

  • One module: Pilot a new approach in one module before rolling out
  • One assessment: Add an oral component to an essay, or require annotated outline before final draft
  • One cohort: Try new approach with one cohort, gather feedback, refine
  • One assessment type: Improve all essays in a module, OR all group projects, before addressing other types
Momentum builds when colleagues see small successes. Small changes that work create confidence to attempt larger redesigns.

Building Support

  • Involve colleagues: Share the framework; discuss together rather than working in isolation
  • Celebrate successes: Share what workedâ€”increased student engagement, reduced time marking, better learning outcomes
  • Learn from setbacks: If something doesn't work, discuss why and adjust
  • Seek external validation: External examiners can help communicate that redesigned assessments remain rigorous

Supporting Your Teaching Team

Staff Development Needs

As you implement assessment redesign, your team may need support in:

  • Understanding the framework: Why we're redesigning, not why we're banning AI
  • Assessing orally or in real-time: Skills for conducting vivas, Q&A sessions, assessments that aren't paper-based
  • Designing rubrics: How to create clear criteria for process-based or collaborative assessments
  • Giving feedback at scale: Strategies for providing meaningful feedback when class sizes are large
  • Addressing student questions: Responding to "Is this fair?" and "Why can't we use AI?"
  • Confidence with assessment design: Staff may feel their current methods are "fine" and need encouragement to try new approaches

Creating a Culture of Assessment Innovation

  • Share the framework and related research with staff
  • Allocate time for discussion and planning (don't expect staff to do this unpaid)
  • Provide examples of successful redesigned assessments
  • Pair experienced staff with those new to redesign
  • Celebrate creative approaches and willingness to experiment
  • Acknowledge that redesign involves learning and iteration
Key message for staff: "We're not asking you to police AI. We're asking you to design assessments that are harder to cheat on and better for learning."

Postplagiarism: Rethinking Academic Integrity

The concept of "plagiarism" is evolving in an AI-enhanced world. "Postplagiarism" describes academic integrity in contexts where traditional plagiarism detection is unreliable.

Traditional Definition

Plagiarism = taking someone else's words or ideas without acknowledgment.

This definition assumes:

  • Clear distinction between "your work" and "not your work"
  • Ability to detect plagiarism via text matching
  • Individual authorship and ownership of ideas

In an AI Era

These assumptions break down:

  • Is AI-generated content "plagiarism" if it's novel text that didn't exist before? (No original author to plagiarize from)
  • Can text-matching tools detect unauthorised AI use reliably? (Noâ€”50%+ false positives)
  • Are students "authoring" if they use AI, or is the AI? (Blurry boundaries)

Postplagiarism Approach

Instead of focusing on detection, focus on transparency and learning:

  • Transparency: Students disclose AI use; tutors discuss what tools were used and why
  • Learning: Assessment design ensures genuine engagement with material (hard to fake with process visibility)
  • Accountability: Clear expectations about what's permitted; consequences for misrepresentation
  • Responsibility: Students develop AI literacy; learn to use tools ethically

This shift is important because:

  • Detection is unreliable: Don't build integrity systems on shaky foundations
  • Technology changes fast: New AI emerges constantly; detection always lags
  • Human judgment matters: Educators understand context; algorithms don't
  • Education is the goal: We want students to learn responsible practice, not just avoid getting caught
Postplagiarism principle: Build integrity through transparent practices, visible learning processes, and student educationâ€”not through detection and punishment.
