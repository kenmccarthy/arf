Framework Overview

The Problem

Since ChatGPT's launch in November 2022, traditional assessment methods‚Äîessays, unsupervised exams, online quizzes‚Äîhave become increasingly vulnerable. Students can access AI tools to produce content that appears original but isn't their own work.

The solution isn't detection. It's redesign.

The Shift

Move from:

  • Detection to Prevention ‚Äî Design out cheating risk
  • Products to Process ‚Äî Make learning visible
  • Surveillance to Transparency ‚Äî Build a culture of integrity
  • Generic to Contextual ‚Äî Adapt to your discipline and students

Central Argument

Assessment must be redesigned structurally, not simply policed through detection.

This framework supports educators in creating robust, fair, valid, and meaningful assessments that ensure students can demonstrate genuine learning in an AI-enhanced world.

7 Tips for Redesigning Your Assessment

Use these tips to redesign one assessment or a whole course

1

Start with Learning Outcomes

Clearly define what you are assessing ‚Äî application of knowledge, critical thinking, problem-solving, analytical or evaluative skills. Determine whether GenAI use supports or undermines those outcomes.

Learn more ‚Üí
2

Be Explicit About AI Permissions

Clearly communicate whether learners are permitted to use GenAI and, if so, to what extent and for which purposes. Where expectations cannot be monitored, redesign the task to make learning visible.

Learn more ‚Üí
3

Balance Higher-Order Thinking with Foundational Knowledge

Design assessments that prioritise analysis, evaluation, and creation, while ensuring students demonstrate essential disciplinary knowledge‚Äîsometimes independently of AI support.

Learn more ‚Üí
4

Use Multi-Faceted Assessment Formats

Combine artefact-based tasks with lower-risk approaches such as oral presentations, peer evaluation, drafts, reflective journals, or viva-style discussions to verify understanding.

Learn more ‚Üí
5

Assess the Process, Not Only the Product

Build in touchpoints or staged components where each part of assessment develops from the previous. Make student thinking visible through drafts, reflections, and interactive elements.

Learn more ‚Üí
6

Incorporate Structured Collaboration

Include opportunities for collaborative problem-solving, peer feedback, or group projects with mechanisms to recognise individual contributions. This reflects authentic practice and makes learning harder to outsource.

Learn more ‚Üí
7

Engage Students in Dialogue About AI and Assessment

Facilitate open conversations about GenAI use, academic integrity, and responsible practice. Involve learners in assessment design when appropriate‚Äîdiscussing criteria or co-creating rubric elements.

Learn more ‚Üí

Key Concepts to Understand

Track 1 AI-Restricted Assessment

AI use is typically not permitted unless the ethical use of an AI tool is purposefully being assessed.

When: Testing foundational knowledge, professional competencies, independent thinking

Example: Supervised clinical simulation, exam, oral defence

Track 2 AI-Integrated Assessment

Responsible use of AI is encouraged and expected.

When: Developing AI literacy, applying knowledge in realistic contexts, critical evaluation of AI outputs

Example: Annotated bibliography with AI use disclosed, project with AI tools documented

Process Visibility

Make how students arrive at conclusions visible‚Äînot just final products.

Methods: Drafts, sketches, reflective journals, staged assessments, oral explanations

Benefit: Detects learning; harder to fake; improves assessment validity

AI Literacy Development

Students develop the ability to engage with AI critically, ethically, and effectively.

Includes: Evaluating outputs, knowing when to use/not use AI, using ethically, understanding limitations

Why: Essential for future careers in AI-saturated world
